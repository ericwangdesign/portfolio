## FIXED COMMITMENTS

These are locked in and non-negotiable — plan everything else around them.

### Friday Night — Drinks at Fred's (DTLA)
After 10 PM, at a friend's apartment in Downtown LA. This anchors Friday evening and means you want to be in or near DTLA by 9:30–10 PM. Everything you do Friday should route toward Downtown, not away from it.

### Friday Dinner — 洞庭鲜 (Dong Ting Xian)
**727 E Valley Blvd, San Gabriel, CA 91776**
Hunan-style Chinese — water-boiled fish, fermented black bean dishes, dry-fried meats. Spicy, intense, night market atmosphere. Not Cantonese, not American-Chinese — proper regional Hunan cooking. 

Important geography note: San Gabriel is ~42 minutes from the hotel and in the opposite direction from DTLA. So Friday routing looks like: Hotel → (SGV for dinner ~7–8 PM) → DTLA (for drinks 10 PM+). That's a ~25-min drive from SGV to DTLA. Manageable but you're not backtracking to the hotel between dinner and drinks — plan your bags accordingly.

Hours: Mon–Sat 11 AM–9:30 PM, Sun 11 AM–9 PM. Dinner at 7–7:30 PM gives comfortable time to eat, get to DTLA, and find parking before 10.

This commitment is marked "not fixed" — meaning if logistics get complicated (arriving late Thursday and needing a low-key night, or wanting to save this for Saturday), it's movable. But it's the plan for now.

### Saturday Lunch — OC & Lau Restaurant
**10130 Garden Grove Blvd, Garden Grove, CA 92844**
Vietnamese seafood. Famous specifically for 18 types of escargot. No reservations. Very local Vietnamese-American crowd — not a trendy spot, just genuinely good. 

Critical detail: This is in Orange County, not Los Angeles. Garden Grove is 38 minutes south of the hotel. So Saturday means driving south to OC for lunch, then driving back north to LA for the afternoon and evening. Plan accordingly — OC is not on the way to anything in LA. Arrive by 11 AM–noon to avoid a significant wait (they don't take reservations). Closed Tuesdays.

## ART AND EXHIBITIONS

These are the things actually worth planning your days around.

### Hospital of Emotions ⚠ CLOSES JULY 31
**2131 W 3rd St, Los Angeles, CA 90057**
**$58 · Timed entry · Daily 10 AM–8:10 PM**

70 artists have installed work across 80+ rooms of a real decommissioned hospital, each room treating a different emotion. You check in at a reception desk, receive actual intake paperwork, move through the hospital floor by floor collecting stamps, and are discharged at the end with a completed chart. It's theatrical in a way that feels earned rather than gimmicky — the hospital architecture does real work. 

Budget 2 hours minimum. Sells out. **Pre-book at hospitalofemotions.com before you go.** If you're traveling any time in June or July, this should be on the shortlist — it closes July 31 and won't come back.

Location is near Koreatown / MacArthur Park area — about 32 minutes from the hotel.

---

### DATALAND — World's First AI Art Museum 🆕
**100 S Grand Ave, Downtown LA (Frank Gehry's The Grand LA)**
**Tickets required · Tue–Fri 11 AM–10 PM · Sat–Sun 10 AM–10 PM**

Co-founded by Refik Anadol (the AI artist known for large-scale data sculptures). 25,000 sqft of immersive generative environments — you're inside the art, not looking at it. The inaugural exhibition "Machine Dreams: Rainforest" runs through January 2027 and uses machine learning trained on rainforest data to create living, breathing visual ecosystems. You can interact with some installations to influence what the AI generates.

This literally just opened on June 20, 2026 — you would be among the first visitors. Right next to The Broad and MOCA. Budget 1.5–2 hours.

Website: dataland.art

---

### Yoko Ono: Music of the Mind — The Broad
**221 S Grand Ave, Downtown LA**
**$21 · Free Thursday 5–8 PM · Through Oct 11, 2026**

Her first solo museum exhibition in Southern California, organized in collaboration with Tate Modern, London. The key thing to understand about this show is that it's participatory — you're not just looking at art, you're completing it. Visitors hammer nails into a canvas (her Nail Painting), write wishes on paper and tie them to olive trees outside (Wish Tree for Los Angeles), and climb a ladder to read the word "YES" on the ceiling (Ceiling Painting/YES Painting). These aren't gimmicks — they're her original instruction works from the 1950s and 60s, and the show traces her whole career from Fluxus through her anti-war campaigns with John Lennon.

If you can time it for a Thursday, entry is free 5–8 PM. Otherwise $21. Budget 1.5 hours.

---

### Hauser & Wirth Los Angeles — FREE
**901 E 3rd St, Arts District**
**Free · Tue–Sun 11 AM–6 PM · Through August 16**

One of the top commercial galleries in the world, and the LA location is genuinely impressive — massive converted industrial space in the Arts District. Current show: Keith Tyson's "The Generative Universe," spanning three decades of paintings, sculptures, and mixed-media work. Tyson programmed a device in the early 90s that generates prompts for him to execute by hand — the show traces that process over 30 years, which ends up being a surprisingly interesting meditation on authorship, creativity, and systems (thematically relevant to DATALAND).

Rooftop garden is worth going to alone — views over Downtown. Free entry, no tickets needed. Natural anchor for an Arts District afternoon — ROW DTLA is a 10-minute walk.

---

### Hammer Museum — FREE
**10899 Wilshire Blvd, Westwood**
**Free · Tue–Sun 11 AM–6 PM (Thu until 8 PM)**

UCLA-affiliated and perpetually underrated. Closest major museum to the hotel (22 minutes). Two strong concurrent shows:

"Several Eternities in a Day: Form in the Age of Living Materials" (through Aug 23) — 22 artists from across the Americas working with living materials: clay, cacao, achiote, cochineal, stone. The show is about how these materials carry cultural memory and what happens when contemporary artists work with them.

"Arthur Jafa: The White Album" (through Aug 30) — a 30-minute experimental film that is one of the most talked-about art films of the decade. Jafa's work examines how visual media transmits the power, beauty, and violence embedded in Black American culture. Heavy, beautiful, essential.

Both free. Good option for a Friday morning or Sunday before driving home.

---

### MOCA Grand Avenue
**250 S Grand Ave, Downtown LA**
**$18 · Through Sept 2026**

Museum of Contemporary Art, right next to The Broad. "Afterlives" (through Sept 20) shows MOCA's own collection from the 1940s–70s — a survey of postwar American and international art. Not the most exciting programming on this list, but if you're already doing The Broad + DATALAND on Grand Ave, MOCA is a 2-minute walk and rounds out the afternoon.

## NEIGHBORHOODS TO EXPLORE

These aren't "attractions" — they're places to wander, shop, eat, and stumble into things. The distinction from tourist LA is that you're not going to see something, you're going somewhere.

### Culver City Arts District
**Washington Blvd between National and Fairfax; La Cienega between Venice and Fairfax**

You're staying here. Galleries include Jeffrey Deitch, Blum & Poe, ThinkSpace, BLK/MRKT, WWA Gallery. USA Today named it one of the top-10 arts districts in the country. Most galleries are open Tue–Sat 11–6. No need to schedule this — just walk out of the hotel and turn left.

---

### Abbot Kinney Boulevard, Venice
**10 minutes from hotel**

The closest neighborhood to the hotel. A walkable strip of indie boutiques, design shops, and cafes running through Venice, a few blocks from the beach. Circular Library is a small Korean-run boutique with interesting selection; Fellow Coffee is visually one of the best-designed coffee shops in the city; Goodies has quality home goods and small objects.

Walk to Venice Beach at the end — it's noticeably calmer and less crowded than Santa Monica. Less commercial than it was 5 years ago. Good morning option: coffee on Abbot Kinney, then walk to the beach before heading east.

---

### Silver Lake
**~28 minutes from hotel · Sunset Blvd corridor**

The neighborhood most consistently recommended by people who actually live in LA as the best place to shop and wander. The density of interesting stores on the Sunset Blvd stretch is high.

Key stops:
- **Rare Reserve** — archive vintage at a level that's rare in LA. Not thrift vintage — archive vintage. You might find 2002 Gucci, deadstock Helmut Lang, things that aren't available anywhere else. Serious pickers come here.
- **Mohawk General Store** — elevated retail, stocks Acne Studios, APC, Paloma Wool, and independent designers. Clean, airy, well-curated.
- **Clare V.** — LA-born accessories brand, minimalist and good.
- **Tartine** — the SF sourdough bakery's LA outpost. Go early (bread sells out). Good coffee.

---

### Highland Park / York Blvd
**~38 minutes from hotel · Most local of the artsy neighborhoods**

York Blvd is the main street of Highland Park, a neighborhood with a long Chicano history that has become one of LA's most concentrated artist communities. The architecture is Arts & Crafts from around 1895 — an interesting built environment to walk through. Artist-run gallery spaces open on weekends, indie record shops, vintage stores, coffee places without a line.

Less polished and more genuinely local than Silver Lake. Sunday has a farmers market with produce from local farms and handmade goods. If you want the feeling of being somewhere real in LA rather than a curated version of it, this is the neighborhood.

---

### Frogtown (Elysian Valley)
**~32 minutes from hotel · Between Silver Lake and Atwater Village, on the LA River**

Almost nobody tells you about Frogtown unless you know someone who lives there. It's a small district of repurposed M2 manufacturing buildings that became artist studios. The LA River walkway runs along the edge and functions as an open-air street art gallery — murals change every few months.

There are no restaurants or boutiques to speak of, and that's the point. It's a working artist community that you can walk through on a weekend when studios are open. The most raw and least performative art neighborhood in LA. Pair with Atwater Village or Silver Lake since they're adjacent.

---

### Arts District, Downtown LA
**~32 minutes from hotel**

The industrial-to-arts-district conversion LA is known for. The anchor is Hauser & Wirth (901 E 3rd St), but the district extends in all directions. ROW DTLA (777 S Alameda) is a massive converted factory complex with Bodega — a cult streetwear shop with no exterior signage, you have to find the door — and Friday Gallery, which focuses on multicultural contemporary artists.

The mural scene is real and constantly changing. Artists like Retna, Shepard Fairey, and DEFER have major works here. Smorgasburg runs here every Sunday 10 AM–4 PM. Walking distance from the Grand Ave museum cluster.

---

### Atwater Village
**~33 minutes from hotel**

A small, dense strip between Silver Lake and Glendale. Jacknife Records & Tapes is the main reason to come — deep used vinyl inventory, knowledgeable staff, well-organized. The Social Type is a small gift shop with a good eye. Small Sunday farmers market. Pairs naturally with Frogtown since they're adjacent.

---

### Melrose Ave / West Hollywood
**~22 minutes from hotel**

Good daytime shopping stretch — vintage boutiques, streetwear, and independent brands along Melrose. Sunset Strip is more of a late-night and music history destination than a shopping destination. Neither is as dense or as interesting as Silver Lake for actual shopping, but they're on the way to Silver Lake from the hotel if you're driving east, and worth a 45-minute pass-through.

## FOOD AND DRINK

### Fixed (see above)
- **Friday dinner:** 洞庭鲜 (San Gabriel)
- **Saturday lunch:** OC & Lau (Garden Grove)

### Recommended

**Tartine** (Silver Lake) — Sourdough, pastries, coffee. Go early. Best paired with a Silver Lake morning.

**Great White** (Larchmont Village) — Sunday brunch. Australian-inflected, good coffee, great food. Always a wait — arrive by 9:30 AM or accept 20–30 minutes. The Larchmont Sunday farmers market is right there.

**Smorgasburg @ ROW DTLA** — Every Sunday, 10 AM–4 PM, 777 S Alameda. 30+ rotating food vendors. Free to enter. Good way to eat lunch if you're doing an Arts District afternoon on Sunday. Summer 2026 has Ice Cream Alley starting July 6.

**Grand Central Market** — 317 S Broadway, Downtown. Indoor market with a mix of traditional stalls and newer food vendors. Tacos Al Pastor are the classic move: $3.50 each, charcoal-roasted pork with pineapple. Good for a quick cheap meal if you're in Downtown.

**Awan** (Venice) — Small dessert spot near Abbot Kinney. Cream puffs, salted caramel. The green shopfront. Worth it if you're already in the neighborhood.

**Sweet Rose Creamery** — Handmade ice cream with daily-rotating flavors. Several locations. Good.

**Go Get Em Tiger** (Larchmont Village) — The coffee shop. Salted Dark Chocolate Mocha. If you're doing Larchmont.

**Fellow** (Abbot Kinney) — Beautifully designed coffee shop. The pour-overs are good. Nice place to sit.

## EVENTS

### Cinespia at Hollywood Forever Cemetery
**6000 Santa Monica Blvd · Saturdays in July · $25–35**

Films screened on the lawn of a working celebrity cemetery (Rudolph Valentino, Cecil B. DeMille, Jayne Mansfield, Johnny Ramone are buried there). Bring blankets, bring food, bring wine — it's all allowed. DJ set before the film, photo booth, the whole production. 25-minute drive from the hotel.

2026 lineup: Pulp Fiction (July 11), Rosemary's Baby (July 18), Edward Scissorhands (July 25).

This is one of the most genuinely local LA experiences available. No tour groups, no out-of-towners — just Angelenos who've been doing this since the series started. If your Saturday lands in July, this is a strong evening anchor.

## KEY THINGS THAT MIGHT SURPRISE YOU

**DATALAND literally just opened** (June 20, 2026). You'd be going in the opening weeks. That's exciting but also means potential lines and first-run technical issues. Worth it.

**The Broad and DATALAND are next door to each other** — and MOCA is one block away. This is the most efficient museum clustering in the city right now.

**OC & Lau is in Orange County, not LA.** The name sounds like it's in LA. It's a 38-minute drive south and requires you to drive back north for everything else on Saturday. Plan the day with that in mind — OC at lunch, then everything heading north toward the hotel.

**洞庭鲜 is in the opposite direction from DTLA.** If you do dinner there Friday, you're driving past your hotel on the way from SGV to DTLA. Not a dealbreaker, but worth knowing.

**Culver City Arts District is at your doorstep.** Most people staying in Culver City don't realize they're in one of the best gallery districts in the country. Worth at least a 30-minute walk.

**Frogtown gets overlooked** even by LA residents. It's not Instagram-optimized and there's nothing to buy. That's exactly why it's worth going to.

**The Hammer is free and genuinely excellent.** It almost never shows up in trip recommendations because it's in Westwood and adjacent to UCLA. Both current shows are strong.
